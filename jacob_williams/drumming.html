Rudimental Percussion

Rudimental percussion refers to a style of playing that is dependent upon certain patterns of stickings and rhythms. This type of percussion is most commonly seen in marching bands and drum corps. High school marching band is where my relationship with rudimental drumming both began and ended. I played for four years, marching bass drumm for one year and snare drum for 3 years. My high school was fortunate enough to be able to march in several national parades as well as competing each fall on a national level. After high school I breifly considered continuing my rudimental percussion career by joining a drum corps, but attending Yale didn't leave enough time for the rigorous practice this would have required.

Concert Percussion

Concert percussion refers to the traditional symphonic and orchestral styles of percussoin that are often heard in concert halls. I've played in some for of concert or sypmhonic band since I was 9 years old, and have also been a part of various percussion ensembles that seek to create full musical pieces with a solely percussive instrumentatio. It is in this setting that I learned to play dozens of percussion instruments: marimbas, timpani, cymbals, vibraphones, chimes, and djembes, just to name a few.

Drumset

Perhaps the most socially glorified form of drumming, drumkits or drum sets are most commonly seen in jazz and rock bands. I started learning to play a drumkit when I was 11 years old and have played for several cover bands as well as a number of praise and jazz bands.
